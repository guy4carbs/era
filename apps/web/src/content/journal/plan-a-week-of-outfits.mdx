To plan a week of outfits is to decide what you will wear before the week starts, so
getting dressed each morning becomes a two-second choice instead of a
standing-in-front-of-the-closet debate. You are not scripting every detail of five
days; you are removing the friction from five ordinary mornings.

The method below takes about fifteen minutes, once a week — a calm Sunday reset — and
it works entirely from the closet you already own. There is nothing to buy and
nothing to perfect. Era's job is to hold the plan and to help when you get stuck; the
plan itself stays refreshingly low-effort.

## Start with the week you actually have

Before you touch a single outfit, look at what the week holds. Glance at your
calendar — which days are ordinary, which lean more casual, which need something
sharper — and check the forecast, because a cold, wet Thursday changes what you'll
want to reach for. In Era, Ovi already factors the weather into her daily
suggestions, but a quick look yourself sets the frame.

Then rough out the shape of the week, not the details. Maybe it is three regular
days, one relaxed one, and one that calls for something more considered. You are not
choosing outfits yet — you are counting the kinds of day you need to dress for. That
count is the whole plan in miniature.

Doing this first also stops you from over-preparing. If only one day genuinely needs
a sharper look, you do not have to think hard about the other four; they can lean on
pieces you wear without a second thought. Matching effort to the day is what keeps a
weekly plan from quietly becoming a chore.

## Anchor each day with one piece

The fastest way to build a look is to start from a single decision. For each day,
pick one anchor — a jacket, a pair of trousers, a dress — and let the rest of the
outfit gather around it. Anchoring is quicker than assembling from scratch because
one strong choice narrows all the others, and it keeps you from staring at the whole
closet trying to build from nothing.

Work through the week this way and you will have five anchors before you have five
finished outfits — which is most of the thinking done.

## Build five looks on the canvas

Now put them together. In Era you compose each outfit on the outfit canvas, dragging
in pieces you own and saving the look, so it is waiting for you on the day. Build
your five, one per day, from those anchors.

When one refuses to come together, this is where Ovi earns her place: ask her, and she
will suggest complete outfits from your closet and fill the gaps around an anchor you
have already chosen. You stay in charge of the plan; she just does the fiddly pairing
when you would rather not. Save the ones you like, and the week is essentially set.

## Leave room for one wildcard

Do not plan all seven days to the last button. A week that is scheduled too tightly
starts to feel like a uniform, and the first disruption — a change of plan, a change
of mood — knocks the whole thing over. Plan five looks and leave a day or two open on
purpose.

That open space is not a failure of planning; it is the part that keeps planning
sustainable. Some mornings you will want to decide in the moment, and having room for
that is what makes the structure worth keeping the next week.

## Log what you wear, and let repeats be fine

At the end of the day, log what you actually wore. It takes a second, and it does two
things: it keeps your record honest, and it teaches Era what earns its place in your
rotation, so future suggestions lean on the looks that work.

And let repeats be fine. Wearing the same reliable outfit twice in a week is not a
failure of imagination — it is a sign you have found something that works, which is
the entire point. The goal was never novelty for its own sake. It was fewer decisions
and more days you feel put together, and a good outfit does not stop being good
because you wore it on Tuesday.

## Why a plan beats willpower

Deciding what to wear is a small choice you make under time pressure, half-awake,
every single morning. Moving that decision to a calm fifteen minutes on Sunday is not
about discipline; it is about doing the thinking when thinking is easy. This is the
habit Era's [outfit planner](/outfit-planner) is built around — a repeatable weekly
rhythm rather than a daily scramble.

It also compounds with a wardrobe you know well. A tighter, more intentional closet
makes the weekly plan almost automatic, because there are fewer, better pieces to
combine. If you are drawn to timeless staples, a
[classic style guide](/styles/classic) shows the pieces that anchor a week without
much effort; if your days run more active, an
[athleisure style guide](/styles/athleisure) does the same for how you actually move
through them. Either way, planning a week gets easier the more your closet reflects
how you really live.

Start with one week. Five looks, one wildcard, and a Sunday you can repeat.
