Digitizing your closet means turning the clothes you already own into a set of
clean digital cards — one per piece, its background removed, tagged by what it is —
so you can see, search, and style from your whole wardrobe in one place. Instead of
a rail you have to dig through, you get an honest picture of everything you have.

In Era there are two ways to build that picture, and neither asks much of you. You
photograph a piece and Era lifts it off its background and tags it — category,
color, brand — on its own. Or, for anything you bought online, you paste the product
link and Era pulls in the image and the details for you. That is really all
"digitize your closet" means here: your real wardrobe, made visible and organized,
without a spreadsheet in sight. The rest of this guide is how to do it calmly.

## Start with the pieces you actually wear

You do not have to catalog everything before this becomes useful. Most people wear a
small share of what they own in any given week, so start there. Photograph the ten
or fifteen things you reach for most — the jeans that always work, the jacket you
live in, the shoes you default to. Within an afternoon you will have a wardrobe that
already reflects how you dress, which is enough for Era to start being helpful.

Working outward from your favorites also keeps the task from feeling like inventory.
You are not documenting your closet for its own sake; you are giving your most-used
pieces somewhere to be seen and combined.

## How to photograph a piece

The photos do not need to be styled or perfect. Lay the item flat or hang it against
a plain wall, in even daylight if you can, and fill most of the frame with the
garment. Era removes the background automatically, so a busy floor or a cluttered
room behind the piece does not matter much — but the cleaner the shot, the cleaner
the cutout.

One piece per photo is the simple rule. Shoot the front, keep it roughly straight,
and move on. You are not retouching; you are capturing.

## Import your online buys by link

For anything you bought online, skip the camera entirely. Paste the product link and
Era fetches the image and the details — the name, the brand, the color — and turns
it into a wardrobe card the same as a photographed piece. It is the fastest way to
add recent purchases, and it means the version of an item in your closet matches the
one the retailer photographed.

Between the two methods, most closets come together quickly: link-import the things
still findable online, photograph the rest.

## Let the tags do the sorting

As each piece comes in, Era tags it by category, color, and brand, so your closet
organizes itself as you go. You are not naming files or building folders. If a tag
is off — a color read as grey that you would call taupe — you can adjust it, but you
rarely need to. The point of the tags is that later, when you or Ovi are building an
outfit, the pieces can be found by what they are.

## Curate as you go — you do not need everything

A digitized closet is more useful when it is honest than when it is exhaustive. You
do not need to photograph every pair of socks or the shirt you keep meaning to
donate. If a piece has not earned a place in your week, it probably does not need a
place in your wardrobe app either. Add what you actually wear, and let the rest stay
in the drawer.

This is also a quiet way to notice what you own too much of, and what you are
missing — which is part of why the afternoon is worth it.

## Keep it current

The one habit that keeps a digital closet valuable is adding new pieces as they
arrive. When you buy something, import it by link or snap a photo before it
disappears into the rotation. A wardrobe that drifts out of date slowly stops being
trustworthy; one that stays current stays useful. It takes seconds per piece, which
is the whole trick — small and continuous beats a big catch-up later.

## What a digitized closet actually unlocks

Once your wardrobe lives in Era, a few things become possible that a physical rail
never allowed.

You can finally see everything you own at once — including the pieces pushed to the
back that you forget you have. That alone tends to change how you shop.

It becomes the foundation of your [virtual wardrobe](/virtual-wardrobe), which is
what lets Ovi, Era's AI stylist, build you a daily outfit from clothes you actually
own — weather-aware, and never a piece you do not have. You can also compose looks
yourself on the outfit canvas and save them, and gather your favorites into "eras,"
the style chapters that mark how your taste shifts over time.

A complete closet also makes your gaps honest. Because Era can see what you have, it
can tell the difference between wanting something new and needing it. If you lean
clean and pared-back, a [minimalist style guide](/styles/minimalist) will show you
the few pieces that most often complete that wardrobe; if your taste runs more
timeless, a [classic style guide](/styles/classic) does the same. Either way the
rule holds: shop your own closet first, and buy only for a real gap.

That is the quiet payoff of digitizing your closet. Not a prettier app, but fewer
confused mornings, fewer duplicate purchases, and a clearer sense of what you
already have to work with.
